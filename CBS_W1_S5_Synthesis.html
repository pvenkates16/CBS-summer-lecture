What Day 1 gave us — the measurement vocabulary

Every AI deployment decision in this session traces back to concepts from S1

Precision & Recall
The governance implication: who bears the cost of each error type? Misclassifying a loan applicant has legal consequences. The threshold decision is a policy decision.
Data Quality
Governance exists because data fails in four ways: bias, drift, volume, quality gaps. Governance is the systematic response to those failures.
Overfitting
A model that only works on training data is ungovernable in production — it will fail when the world changes. Model monitoring is the governance response to drift.
Confusion matrix
FP and FN rates are the raw material of AI regulation. The EU AI Act essentially asks: what are your error rates, and who is harmed by them?
ML workflow
The pipeline from raw data to deployed model is the governance audit trail. Lineage requires being able to trace every output back through every pipeline stage.

What Day 1, Session 2 gave us — new failure modes

NLP and GenAI introduced failure modes that classical ML governance wasn't designed for

Classical ML failure modes
Bias in training data
Amazon hiring AI · measurable, auditable, fixable
Drift
COVID demand model · detectable with monitoring
Wrong threshold
Fraud / cancer screening · policy decision, not technical
GenAI / Agentic additional failure modes
Hallucination
Confidently wrong — not a bug, a feature of prediction. RAG mitigates but doesn't eliminate.
No explainability
You cannot trace why a specific output was produced. GDPR Article 22 requires this for automated decisions.
Cascading agent errors
95%^10 = 60% end-to-end. Each step without human review compounds. Governance must specify where humans re-enter.

What Session 3 gave us — the trust layer

Governance is not a constraint on AI. It is the infrastructure that makes AI trustworthy enough to deploy.

Data Quality
The 80% rule. Bias, drift, volume, labelling. Without this, ML produces garbage confidently.
Lineage & Metadata
Track every transformation from raw data to model output. Required for audit, regulation, and explainability.
Ethics & Compliance
EU AI Act risk tiers. GDPR automated decision rules. Fairness metrics. The legal framework around AI outputs.
Security & Access
Who can read what data. Who can train what model. Who can deploy what agent. The permission layer.
EU AI Act (2024)
Risk-tiered regulation. High-risk AI (credit, hiring, justice) requires conformity assessment before deployment.
GDPR Art. 22
Right to explanation for automated decisions. Requires lineage + human override capability.
Basel IV / BCBS 239
Banks must evidence data quality and lineage in all risk models. Same requirements, different regulator.

What Session 4 gave us — the threat and the horizon

Quantum computing is simultaneously a present security emergency and a future capability accelerant

⚠ The present threat
Shor's algorithm breaks RSA & ECC
A fault-tolerant quantum computer with ~1M qubits (likely by 2030–2035) can break the encryption protecting most of the internet, banking systems, and government communications.
Harvest now, decrypt later: adversaries are already collecting encrypted data today to decrypt when quantum computers arrive. The threat is present even before the capability exists.
NIST: deprecate RSA by 2030, prohibit by 2035. NSA: national security systems quantum-safe by 2027.
◈ The future capability
Quantum accelerates AI
Quantum ML — solving optimisation problems in ML training exponentially faster. 5–10 years.
Quantum optimisation — portfolio, logistics, drug discovery. Grovers algorithm quadratic speedup on search. 5–10 years.
Quantum simulation — molecular modelling, materials science, climate. Near-term for specific domains.
Quantum GenAI — quantum-native transformers. 10+ years, highly speculative.

The integrated architecture — what organisations need now

Three domains. One architecture. Hover each node and edge to explore the dependencies.

Organisations that treat these as three separate teams with separate roadmaps will struggle. The lines between them are not optional.

What this means for your career

You will work with all three of these domains. The question is whether you see them as connected.

The organisations that will struggle
• AI team, governance team, and security team with separate roadmaps that never meet
• Deploying GenAI without lineage infrastructure — cannot explain outputs to regulators
• Treating quantum as "someone else's problem" — collecting sensitive data under RSA encryption with 10-year retention
• Adding governance after deployment — technically possible, commercially and reputationally very costly
The organisations that will lead
• AI strategy built on a governance foundation from day one — governance is a competitive advantage, not a compliance cost
• Data lineage infrastructure that makes both AI explainability and regulatory audit tractable
• Quantum migration programme already running — classified sensitive data, hybrid encryption deployed
• People who can speak all three languages — you, after this course

Week 2 is about how organisations navigate this in practice. You now have the map.
