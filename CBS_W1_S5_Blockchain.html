Why does blockchain exist?

One problem. Two thousand years of solutions. One new architecture.

The problem
How do two parties who don't trust each other transact safely?
If I send you digital money, what stops me from sending the same money to someone else? Digital files can be copied. Digital money needs something a physical coin has: you can only spend it once.
→
Traditional answer (2000 years old)
Trusted intermediary — a bank, a notary, a clearinghouse — keeps the authoritative record. Everyone trusts the institution.
Works well. But: single point of control, single point of failure, cost, speed.
Blockchain answer (2008)
No central authority. Instead: a shared ledger replicated across thousands of nodes. Rules enforced by cryptography and consensus, not institutions.
Slower and more expensive to run. But: no single controller, no single point of failure.

Blockchain only wins where you specifically need to remove the trusted intermediary — either because none exists, or because participants don't want to cede control to a single party.

How blockchain works — one slide

No mathematics needed. Three mechanisms do the work.

🔗
Hash chain
Every block contains the fingerprint of the previous block
Change one transaction and you change the block's fingerprint — which breaks every subsequent block. Tampering is computationally detectable.
🌐
Distributed ledger
Thousands of identical copies held by independent nodes
To falsify a record you would need to rewrite the chain on more than half the network simultaneously — practically impossible on large public chains.
🗳️
Consensus
The network agrees on the valid version of the ledger
Proof of Work (Bitcoin — energy intensive), Proof of Stake (Ethereum — efficient), or BFT variants (enterprise chains — fast, permissioned).
Smart contracts — code that executes automatically when conditions are met. "If payment received AND goods delivered THEN release funds." No intermediary needed to enforce the contract.
The key insight: the rules are in the protocol, not in an institution. Bitcoin has never been hacked at the protocol level — though the exchanges that hold bitcoin have been.

Types of blockchain

Not all blockchains are Bitcoin. The relevant question for finance is always: who can participate?

Public / Permissionless
Anyone can join, read, write, validate
No central authority. Maximum decentralisation. Slow (~7 tx/sec for Bitcoin vs ~1,700 for Visa). All transactions visible.
Examples:
Bitcoin Ethereum Solana
Use: digital assets, DeFi, crypto payments
Private / Permissioned
Known participants only — invitation required
Faster (~1,000s tx/sec). Privacy controls. Regulators know who participants are. Most financial applications use this model.
Examples:
Hyperledger Fabric R3 Corda
Use: interbank settlement, trade finance, CBDCs
Consortium
Group of organisations share control
Multiple institutions jointly operate the network. Balance between decentralisation and governance. No single controller.
Examples:
RippleNet Quorum (JPMorgan)
Use: cross-border payments, supply chain
🆕 Beyond blockchain — DAG-based DLTs
Hedera Hashgraph
Directed Acyclic Graph, not a chain. Uses virtual voting consensus. 10,000+ tx/sec. Governed by a council of global enterprises (Google, IBM, Boeing). Used by payment networks and tokenisation platforms.
IOTA
Designed for machine-to-machine micropayments and IoT. Feeless transactions. Being adopted for supply chain traceability and smart cities. No miners — every participant validates.
The common thread
All solve the same problem: trustless distributed ledger without a central authority. The underlying mechanism differs — chain vs DAG vs tangle. For finance, the governance structure (who runs it?) often matters more than the technology.

Where blockchain is actually used in finance

Separate the hype from the production deployments

✓ Working in production
Cross-border payments
RippleNet used by Santander, SBI Holdings. Reduces correspondent banking steps from 3-4 days to seconds.
Trade finance
Letters of credit on blockchain (Contour, formerly Voltron). Reduces processing from 5-10 days to 24 hours.
Tokenised assets
BlackRock BUIDL fund ($500M+ AUM), Franklin Templeton money market — real assets on public blockchain.
Central Bank Digital Currencies
50+ countries piloting or launched. China e-CNY, ECB digital euro in development. Most use permissioned blockchain.
✗ Where it has not delivered
Supply chain provenance
Walmart food safety, Maersk TradeLens (shut down 2022). The data going on-chain is still entered by humans — blockchain doesn't solve the garbage-in problem.
Most retail banking
Traditional databases are 100x faster and far cheaper to operate. Blockchain only wins where you need to remove the trusted intermediary — most banks are happy to be that intermediary.
DeFi at scale
Genuine innovation in protocol design but $3.8B lost to hacks in 2022 alone. Smart contract bugs are catastrophic — code is law, including the bugs.

Future outlook

Three threads, three different timelines

Near-term (1–3 years)
Tokenisation of real-world assets
Bonds, equities, real estate, private credit on-chain. BlackRock, Fidelity, Franklin Templeton already live. Larry Fink: "tokenisation of financial assets is the next generation for markets."
Expected: $10–16 trillion in tokenised assets by 2030 (McKinsey, BCG)
Medium-term (3–7 years)
CBDCs and programmable money
Central bank digital currencies move from pilot to live. Programmable money — automatic tax collection, conditional payments, expiring stimulus. The monetary system becomes software.
134 countries representing 98% of global GDP exploring CBDCs (Atlantic Council, 2024)
Long-term (7+ years)
Convergence with AI and quantum
AI agents transacting autonomously on blockchain rails. Quantum-resistant cryptography baked into new chains. The trust infrastructure of a future where machines transact with machines.
Speculative — but the S5 synthesis graph connects all three

Blockchain is not replacing the financial system. It is becoming part of the infrastructure of the financial system — especially where trust between institutions needs to be provable rather than assumed.
