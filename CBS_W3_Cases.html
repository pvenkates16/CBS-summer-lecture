Governance Question 1

Purpose Drift in Healthcare

Can surveillance infrastructure be "benign" in new domains? Palantir's technology was designed for law enforcement and counter-terrorism. Now it's being deployed in healthcare. Is this innovation or purpose creep?

Context

Palantir: Then vs. Now

Original domain (2003-2020): Law enforcement, counter-terrorism, surveillance. Company worked with ICE on deportation analytics (2017-2018). Mission: "Find patterns in data nobody else can see."

New domain (2020-2024): Healthcare system optimization. Claim: "Help hospitals run efficiently." Using same graph database technology but new domain. Patient records + operational data = hospital optimization.

Technology is identical. Domain is different. Ethics might be different too.

Decision Point

You are a hospital CIO in 2024

Palantir offers integrated platform: consolidate patient data, operational data, supply chain. Benefits: 15-20% efficiency gains, faster bed management, reduced redundant tests.

The tension: You know Palantir's ICE history. You know they're powerful at finding patterns. You know data collected for "hospital efficiency" could theoretically be reused for other purposes.

Your decision:

Do you sign the contract? And if yes, what governance guardrails do you require?

Governance Considerations

Questions to ask before signing

  • Use case boundaries: Can you contractually restrict use to "hospital efficiency"? What about fraud detection? Law enforcement requests?
  • Data governance: What data can Palantir store? For how long? Can patients opt out of the integrated system?
  • Law enforcement access: If law enforcement requests patient data, can you refuse? Or does Palantir cooperate?
  • Transparency: Can you audit what Palantir does with your data? Can patients know their data is in the system?
  • Sunset clause: Can you exit the contract if governance boundaries break down?

Judgment Call

There is no "correct" answer

  • Argument for signing: 15-20% efficiency = fewer mistakes, faster care, better outcomes. Patients benefit. Risk mitigation through contracts.
  • Argument against: Once data is integrated, boundaries erode. Palantir's history shows they will cooperate with governments. Patient trust is more valuable than 15% efficiency.
  • Argument for restricted signing: Sign, but demand governance. Use contracts as forcing function. Require annual audits. Demand opt-out rights.
  • What matters: You decide based on your hospital's values and risk tolerance. Can you live with the outcome if your governance fails?

Governance Decision 2 of 2

Post-Quantum Cryptography

Your bank harvests encrypted transactions today that adversaries will decrypt in 2035. Migrate cryptography now or later? A $1B decision with incomplete information.

Timeline: 10–15 years to threat. Migration: 5+ years. Decision: Today.

Threat Model

Harvest-Now, Decrypt-Later (HNDL)

TODAY (2026)

  • ✗ Adversaries monitor your encrypted traffic
  • ✗ Store encrypted financial transactions
  • ✗ Harvest customer data, wire routes, account balances
  • ✗ Cannot decrypt YET (classical computers)
  • ✗ Cost: storage is cheap

2035+ (QUANTUM ERA)

  • ✓ Quantum computers arrive (or sufficient qubits)
  • ✓ Shor's algorithm breaks RSA-2048 in hours
  • ✓ Decrypt 10 years of stored transactions
  • ✓ Extract secrets: customer data, trade strategies, payment flows
  • ✓ Damage is retroactive—data already stolen in 2026

Key insight: The vulnerability exists NOW. The decryption capability arrives LATER. You decide TODAY.

Standards & Regulatory

NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standards (August 2024)

ML-KEM (Key Encapsulation)

CRYSTALS-Kyber variant. Lattice-based. Replaces RSA/ECDH for key exchange. Performance: ≈3% overhead.

ML-DSA (Digital Signature)

CRYSTALS-Dilithium variant. Lattice-based. Replaces RSA/ECDSA for signing. Performance: ≈5% overhead.

SLH-DSA (Stateless Hash-Based)

SPHINCS+ variant. Conservative backup. Slower but extremely well-understood. Fallback if lattice assumptions fail.

Status: NSA mandated migration timeline (2024). CISA issued guidance. SEC reviewing disclosure requirements. Regulators expect migration plans by 2025–2026.

The Governance Decision

You are the Bank's CIO. The Board Asks:

Do we migrate to post-quantum cryptography NOW (cost: $800M–$1.2B over 3 years) or LATER (cost: unknown, risk: retroactive data compromise)?

Timeline pressure: Quantum threat ≈ 10–15 years away. Migration ≈ 5–7 years. Window closes in 3 years if you wait.

Stakeholders at the table

  • CTO: "We can do this. Plan is solid. Costs are realistic."
  • CFO: "This is a $1B bet on a threat 10 years away. Opportunity cost?"
  • Chief Compliance: "Regulators expect migration plans. Inaction = audit finding."
  • Chief Information Security: "Harvesting is happening now. Every day we delay, we accumulate risk."

Apply the Framework

7 Conditions for Viable Post-Quantum Migration

1. Clear Scope: Cryptography only (not quantum computing itself). Excludes AI/ML crypto.
2. Data Fitness: Current encrypted data IS vulnerable. NIST standards ARE field-tested (years of peer review). Fit is clear.
3. Feedback Loops: Penetration testing, standards compliance testing, post-deployment audits. Measurable.
4. Recovery from Failure: Hybrid deployment (classical + post-quantum in parallel). Rollback path exists. Risky but viable.
5. Right Measurement: Uptime, deployment completion %, incident response time. NOT "quantum-readiness" (too vague).
6. Explainability: NIST standards are public and well-documented. Can explain to board, regulators, customers.
7. Human Backstop: CTO + Board cryptography expert + CISO decision + external audit. High-stakes governance in place.

For Your Judgment

Decisions Without Correct Answers

  • 1. Time Horizon: How do you weigh a $1B cost today against a $10B+ breach risk in 2035? What discount rate do you use?
  • 2. Uncertainty: Quantum timeline is uncertain (could be 8 years, could be 20). How much uncertainty justifies delaying?
  • 3. Competitive Signal: If you migrate now, competitors who wait gain $800M in near-term cashflow. Does that matter?
  • 4. Regulatory Optics: Does the board's decision to migrate protect them from future scrutiny, or lock them into a costly path?
  • 5. Governance Accountability: Who decides? Board? CIO alone? What happens if the decision is wrong?
