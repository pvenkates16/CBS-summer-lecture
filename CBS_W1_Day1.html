Session 1 — after deployment

Distribution shift — when the world moves on

Data drift

The inputs change. A fraud model trained on 2022 patterns sees 2026 patterns. Same task. Different world. Accuracy degrades silently.

Concept drift

The relationship between inputs and outputs changes. What “creditworthy” means shifts after a recession. The model is now optimising for the wrong thing.

The confidence trap

When distribution shift happens, the model does not know. It continues producing high-confidence outputs — because it is still making internally consistent decisions. High confidence does not mean high accuracy.

Connection forward: In Week 2, the Lucas Critique is a specific mechanism of concept drift — deploying a model changes the behaviour it was trained to predict. In Week 3, a logistics AI routes ships into inaccessible ports with high confidence because it has never seen simultaneous multi-corridor disruption.

Formative check

Work through these individually · not graded · click to reveal answer

⊞

Confusion matrix

A fraud model flags 100 transactions. 80 are real fraud. 20 are legitimate. It misses 40 actual fraud cases. What is the model's precision and recall?

▸

Precision = 80/100 = 80%     Recall = 80/(80+40) = 67%
High precision — when it flags, it's usually right. But it misses 1 in 3 real fraud cases. For AML, that recall is too low.

⏳

Data quality

A Danish bank trains a credit model on 2015–2019 data. In 2021 it starts approving loans that default. Name the most likely data failure.

▸

Data drift — COVID changed borrower behaviour: income patterns, employment stability, sector risk all shifted. The model kept predicting from a pre-COVID world it no longer lived in.

🔍

Learning types

You want to segment 2 million customers into natural groups with no predefined categories. Which learning type, and why?

▸

Unsupervised learning — there are no labels. Clustering discovers structure in the data without being told what to find. Supervised learning needs labelled examples.

⚠️

Overfitting

A vendor claims their fraud model achieves 99.8% accuracy. Should you be impressed? What question do you ask first?

▸

Ask: what is the fraud rate in the dataset? If only 0.2% of transactions are fraud, a model that labels everything "legitimate" gets 99.8% accuracy while catching zero fraud. Always ask for precision and recall on imbalanced datasets.

Case: Real-time NLP in financial risk

The problem

Banks, hedge funds, and individual investors are exposed to risk events that appear in news and social media hours or days before they reach structured data feeds.

The NLP solution

Real-time ingestion of news and social media. NER identifies entities (companies, people, geographies). Sentiment and relevance scoring surfaces only the signals that matter.

The outcome

Reduced risk exposure for clients who acted on signals before they moved markets. Improved returns for those using it as an alpha source. Cognitive AI doing exactly what it was designed to do.

This is classification, NER, and sentiment — all cognitive tier — applied to unstructured data at speed.

Under the hood — how NLP reads text

Skip-able reference slide — Prabhu can step through if questions arise

POS tagging

Every word gets a grammatical label: noun, verb, adjective. This tells downstream models what role each word plays — "Apple" as a company noun vs "apple" as a fruit noun.

Apple [ORG] beat [VB] Q3 [DATE] earnings [NN] expectations [NNS]

Dependency parsing

Words have relationships. "Apple beat expectations" has a subject (Apple), verb (beat), and object (expectations). This structure enables meaning extraction from unstructured text.

Used in: contract analysis, regulatory text, earnings call parsing

The transformer architecture makes most of this implicit — but understanding the pipeline helps you ask better questions of NLP vendors

🔢

Confidently wrong

The training cutoff problem — and the fix

LLMs freeze at training time. Two ways to address it — different cost structures.

RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Same LLM. One extra step: look it up before answering. A retrieval system fetches current documents; the LLM synthesises an answer grounded in them.

User: "What did the Fed say about rates last week?"

→ Retrieves last week's statement → synthesises answer

Low cost per query · knowledge stays current · no retraining

Fine-tuning / Retraining

Bake new knowledge into the model weights. Expensive and slow — but the model internalises domain expertise rather than looking it up at runtime.

Frontier model training: $50M–$100M

Fine-tuning on domain data: $10K–$500K

High upfront · knowledge fixed at fine-tune time · better for style/tone

Most enterprise deployments combine both: fine-tune for behaviour, RAG for current knowledge

Agentic AI — benefits, use cases, and pitfalls

Autonomy creates leverage. It also creates new failure modes.

Benefits

⚡ End-to-end automation — multi-step tasks without constant human handoffs

🔄 Adaptive to context — handles ambiguous goals, adjusts mid-task

📈 Scales effort — one agent can run thousands of parallel workflows

Use cases now

🎫 Customer service — reads ticket, checks DB, issues refund, confirms

📊 Financial research — pulls data, runs models, drafts memo

⚖️ Compliance monitoring — flags anomalies, routes for review

Pitfalls

📉 Error compounding — 95% per step × 10 steps = 60% end-to-end

🎯 Goal misalignment — small misread cascades through the chain

🔍 Accountability gap — who owns an autonomous agent's action?

Autonomy is a dial, not a switch. The question is where to set it for each task.

Classification

A bank scans customer emails to flag complaints about fees. Which AI family — NLP, Generative, or Agentic?

▸

NLP — specifically text classification. Reading and classifying existing text is NLP. Generative AI would draft a reply. Agentic AI would draft, send, and escalate.

👻

Hallucination

A lawyer uses an LLM for case research. It returns three citations. Two are real. One doesn't exist. Why — and what should the lawyer have done?

▸

The model predicted a plausible citation, not a real one. LLMs generate statistically likely text, not verified facts. Fix: use RAG grounded in a real legal database, or verify every citation independently.

⚡

Agentic risk

An agentic AI reads documents, checks credit, runs affordability models, and issues loan decisions. At which step should a human be in the loop?

▸

At the decision step — regulated, irreversible, and high-stakes. Issuing a credit decision is a high-risk system under the EU AI Act. Human-in-the-loop is legally required, not optional.

🎲

Compounding errors

An agentic system has 90% accuracy per step across a 7-step workflow. What is the rough end-to-end reliability?

▸

0.9⁷ ≈ 48% — worse than a coin flip. Errors compound multiplicatively. Keep agentic workflows short, add validation checkpoints, and use human review where errors cascade undetected.
