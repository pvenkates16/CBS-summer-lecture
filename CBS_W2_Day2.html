Amazon AI Hiring Tool

What was attempted

Build "the holy grail of recruitment": an automated CV-screening tool that would take 100 CVs and return the top 5 candidates. Amazon had 10 years of historical hiring decisions — CVs plus outcomes — which appeared to be an ideal training dataset.

The goal

Score incoming CVs 1–5. Surface top candidates automatically without human review.

Why it seemed reasonable

Historical hiring data is a natural dataset. Learning from past successful hires seems like the right approach.

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

Method employed

  • Supervised learning on 10 years of historical hiring decisions. Labels: hired / not hired.
  • Model trained to identify patterns in CVs that predicted a job offer.
  • No quality gate at data ingestion — no audit of whether hired/not hired labels were themselves unbiased.
  • No diversity review of the training set before training began.
  • Three years of development before bias was discovered in 2015.

The framing nobody questioned

"Find candidates who look like our successful past hires."

Amazon AI Hiring Tool

How would you have rescued it?

What was actually done

Amazon scrapped the tool in 2017. It was never deployed at scale. Reuters reported the story in 2018. No regulatory action. No public disclosure until the leak.

Was it enough?

The right call was to scrap it. But scrapping without disclosure means no industry learning. The same mistake can be made elsewhere.

  • Label audit: Before training, audit labels for bias. Are hired/not hired outcomes equal across gender, ethnicity, and age? If not, the labels cannot be used as ground truth.
  • Reframing: "Find candidates who look like past hires" → "Find candidates whose skills match the role requirements." Different objective, different training data.
  • Disclosure: Should companies disclose when they discover bias in AI tools — even tools never deployed? Under the EU AI Act, high-risk AI requires documented bias testing. Would that have changed this?

Google Photos

What was attempted

Google launched Photos in May 2015 with AI-powered automatic image tagging — grouping and labelling photos by subject matter. The computer vision model was trained to recognise objects, animals, and people. The training data had not adequately represented darker skin tones. Google's workforce was approximately 2% Black at launch.

The goal

Automatically organise photos by content. Label images without user input.

What was missed

No QA testing on darker skin tones. No diverse testing team. No check before launch.

What saved it

One tweet from one user. Google removed the label — not the bias.

Save type

Lucky. The underlying model was never fixed.

Design challenge

  • Pre-deployment bias testing: Test model performance across demographic groups before launch. Fail if disparity exceeds threshold. Alciné himself: "A diverse QA team could have caught this if they tested it on themselves."
  • Remediation vs. suppression: Removing a label hides the problem. Fixing the model requires collecting diverse training data and retraining. Why was suppression chosen? What would it have taken to fix the model?
  • Industry accountability: Apple and Microsoft adopted the same workaround. If the industry normalises suppression as a response to bias, what is the governance mechanism that would force remediation instead?

Air Canada

What was attempted

Air Canada deployed an AI chatbot on aircanada.com to handle customer queries at scale — including bereavement fare questions — without human agent involvement. In November 2022, Jake Moffatt used the chatbot to ask about the bereavement discount policy before booking last-minute tickets to Ontario for a family funeral.

The goal

Answer customer policy queries 24/7 without human agents.

What the chatbot did

Generated the most plausible-sounding policy description. Which was wrong.

Air Canada

How would you have rescued it?

What was actually done

Air Canada was ordered to pay CA$812. The chatbot was subsequently removed from the website. No systemic industry response to chatbot liability followed immediately.

Was it enough?

The payment was made. The harm to Moffatt was remediated. The precedent was set. But the underlying issue — deploying GenAI for policy queries without a retrieval layer — remains common practice.

  • RAG architecture: Ground the chatbot in the actual policy document. Any answer about policy must be retrieved from the source, not generated from training patterns.
  • Scope restriction: GenAI chatbots should not answer questions where a wrong answer creates a legal or financial obligation. Policy questions should route to verified content or a human.
  • Disclosure: Should customers be told when they are talking to a chatbot — and that its answers may not reflect actual policy? Is that sufficient?

Pieces Technologies

How would you have rescued it?

What was actually done

Texas AG settlement — five-year disclosure requirement. No criminal charges. System remained deployed during investigation. First enforcement precedent in clinical GenAI accuracy.

Was it enough?

Disclosure requirement is a floor, not a ceiling. It does not require accuracy to improve — only that inaccurate claims be disclosed. Patients remain exposed.

  • Independent benchmarking: Clinical AI accuracy claims should be validated by an independent body using standardised methodology before marketing to hospitals. No self-reporting.
  • Mandatory human review: Every AI-generated clinical note should require explicit clinician sign-off before entering the patient record. The AI drafts; the human certifies.
  • Comparison question: IBM Watson never reached patients. Pieces deployed with real patient data and overstated its accuracy. Which failure is worse — and why?
