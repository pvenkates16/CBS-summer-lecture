Qubits and superposition (interactive)

Explore the 3D Bloch sphere

The sphere shows all possible quantum states of a single qubit. The purple dot is the current state—any point on the surface is valid. North pole is |0⟩, south is |1⟩. Click Measure to collapse it to a definite outcome.

Entanglement (interactive)

Two Bloch spheres, one linked quantum state

Collapsed: Qubit A = |0⟩ → Qubit B = |1⟩

Teal sphere: Qubit A. Coral sphere: Qubit B. Their state dots move in anti-correlation. Press Measure — both collapse to opposite poles simultaneously, no matter the distance.

Quantum interference (interactive)

Cancel wrong answers, amplify correct ones

The blue wave (correct answer) is in phase with itself—it amplifies. The two red waves (wrong answers) are out of phase—they cancel each other. Quantum algorithms choreograph these amplitudes so only correct answers survive. That is how you compute.

But there's a catch

Why you don't have a quantum laptop

❄️
Near absolute zero
Most systems operate at −273°C. Colder than outer space.
🫧
Extreme fragility
Slightest vibration, heat, or EM interference ruins the computation.
⚠️
Error rates
Errors compound quickly. Need thousands of physical qubits per logical qubit.
🏗️
NISQ era
We're in 'noisy intermediate-scale quantum.' Fault tolerance is years away.

Part 3 of 6

When does quantum help?

The honest answer

Quantum computing offers speedup
for a small number of specific problem types.

For everything else, classical computers are better
— and will remain so.

Finance applications worth knowing

What banks and asset managers are exploring — all still experimental

📊
Portfolio optimisation
Best asset mix under constraints. QAOA on NISQ hardware.
🎲
Monte Carlo
Derivative pricing, VaR. Quadratic speedup via amplitude estimation.
🌐
Risk modelling
Simulating many correlated scenarios in parallel.
🔍
Fraud detection
Finding patterns in transaction graphs. Grover-type search.
No production quantum system has beaten a classical computer at any of these tasks at commercial scale. Yet.

Shor's breaks RSA & ECC
Once a fault-tolerant machine reaches ~100K–1M qubits
ECC breaks first
P-256 (most digital signatures, crypto) may need only ~10K qubits
Migration takes 5–10 years
Cloudflare targeting 2029. Large banks: longer.

Why timelines keep slipping

A pattern worth recognising

The forecasts

  • 2015: quantum advantage by 2020
  • 2020: useful quantum by 2025
  • 2025: useful quantum by 2030

Why they slip

  • Engineering challenges harder than expected
  • Error correction requires 1000x physical qubits per logical qubit
  • Classical computing keeps improving — closing some gaps
The CEO question: 'Should we invest now?' For most businesses: no, not directly. Watch the field. Have someone tracking it. Don't bet the strategy on it.

Part 5 of 6

Post-quantum cryptography

The crypto threat

One area where you should pay attention now

Most internet encryption (RSA, ECC) relies on factoring being computationally hard

Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers efficiently on a quantum computer

Once a large enough quantum computer exists, current encryption breaks

We're not there yet — but governments and banks are preparing now

"Harvest now, decrypt later"

An adversary can intercept encrypted data today,
store it,
and decrypt it years from now
when quantum computers are powerful enough.

For secrets that need to stay secret for decades,
the threat is already real.

The post-quantum migration

What organisations should do — the sequence matters

5–10 years for large orgs. Don't wait.

Reflection

Common misconceptions

Click to flip — myth on front, fact on back

MYTH
"Quantum computers are just faster classical computers."
FACT
They solve a different class of problems. For most tasks, classical is and will remain better.
MYTH
"Quantum computing will break all encryption soon."
FACT
10–20 years away. But "harvest now, decrypt later" makes it a present-day concern for long-lived secrets.
MYTH
"My company needs a quantum strategy now."
FACT
You need a crypto migration plan. The quantum strategy can wait. Watch, track, don't bet.

Three things to carry forward

01
Quantum is real but narrow
It helps with a small class of problems — optimisation, simulation, factoring, search. For everything else, classical wins.
02
The crypto threat is the urgent part
"Harvest now, decrypt later" means the migration to post-quantum cryptography should start now — even though fault-tolerant quantum is years away.
03
Hardware → Algorithm → Problem is the matching exercise
When you see a quantum claim, ask: which hardware, which algorithm, which problem shape? If the answer isn't specific, it's probably hype.
